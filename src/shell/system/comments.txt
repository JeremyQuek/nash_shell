
### The "Legit TTY" Architecture

In Linux, a terminal is a pair of file descriptors:

1. **PTY Master:** Your Electron Backend holds this. You read/write to it.
2. **PTY Slave:** Your Shell Process is attached to this. It thinks it's a real hardware terminal.

### How to implement it yourself (The System Calls)

If you are writing your shell launcher in C (or using Node's FFI to call C), here are the steps to "TTY it" properly:

#### 1. Open the Master

Use `posix_openpt(O_RDWR)`. This asks the kernel for a new pseudo-terminal master.

* Call `grantpt()` and `unlockpt()` to prepare the slave side.
* Call `ptsname()` to get the path to the slave (e.g., `/dev/pts/4`).

#### 2. Fork the Child

Call `fork()`.

#### 3. In the Child (The Shell)

Before you `exec` your shell loop:

* **Disconnect from the parent:** Call `setsid()` to create a new session. This is the magic step that makes the process a "session leader."
* **Open the Slave:** Open the path you got from `ptsname()` (e.g., `/dev/pts/4`).
* **Set Controlling Terminal:** Use `ioctl(fd, TIOCSCTTY, 0)`. This tells the kernel, "This TTY is now the boss of this session."
* **Redirect Standard FDs:** Use `dup2` to make the slave FD your `stdin` (0), `stdout` (1), and `stderr` (2).
* **Exec:** Now run your shell process.

#### 4. In the Parent (Electron Backend)

You now have the **Master FD**.

* Anything you `write()` to the Master, the Shell receives as `stdin`.
* Anything the Shell prints to `stdout` appears as something you can `read()` from the Master.

---

### Why this makes `ssh` and `top` work

When `ssh` starts up, it calls an `ioctl` check on its `stdin`.

* **Raw Pipe:** The kernel says "This is a pipe." SSH says "Nope, I need a TTY."
* **PTY Slave:** The kernel says "This is a character device (`/dev/pts/X`)." SSH is happy.

### The "Electron" Difficulty

The challenge with Electron is that Node.js's standard `child_process` module doesn't give you a way to call `setsid()` or `TIOCSCTTY` easily between the `fork` and the `exec`.

**If you want to keep it "Package Free," you have two choices:**

1. **Write a small C wrapper:** Create a 20-line C program that handles the PTY setup and then `execs` your shell. Your Electron app just talks to that C program.
2. **Use Node `fs` on `/dev/ptmx`:** You can manually open `/dev/ptmx` (the master multiplexer) in Node, but you will still struggle to perform the `ioctl` calls without a native C binding.
